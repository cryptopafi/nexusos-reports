Main legitimacy gaps

  • RUO label is not a legal shield. Under EU medicinal-products law, presentation and function matter; human-use, therapeutic, dosing, or clinical-effect framing can move a product toward unauthorized medicinal-product risk.
  • Site still has the classic RUO contradiction: research/clinical language, strong consumer-commerce CTAs, and broad-buyer sales posture in one public surface.
  • 18/30 in-stock catalog products lack public COA entries or explicit absence states.
  • Janoshik links were browser-verified later, but public CTA wording and metadata still need to treat them as verification pages, not direct PDFs.
  • Pricing model is not decision-grade for scaling: only 15/30 rows are complete; VAT and 30 percent marketing reserve make 0/30 SKUs pass the 60 percent margin gate under full reserve.
  • Airtable stock movements are not a complete beginning-to-now ledger; they reconcile current stock only by combining PepInternal baseline, supplier/order evidence, and audit outputs.
  • Broad affiliate, cloaked ad traffic, and MLM-style distribution would create high regulatory/platform risk.
  • Bulgaria is the best current operating-base candidate, but only after counsel and broker validation per SKU/import flow; it must not be treated as a blanket legal safe harbor.
  • Accessories can become a lower-risk cashflow track only if separated from peptide claims and restricted to low-risk storage/organization/cold-chain products first.
  • Ibiza/local distribution can become a legitimacy risk if it creates off-ledger sales, direct cash routes, human-use claims, or informal handoffs. Local physical handoff is allowed only after website/system order and payment confirmation.

Principles

  1. Legitimacy beats velocity. PetraLab should scale only through documented buyer intent, batch proof, compliant copy, traceable stock, and reproducible accounting.
  2. RUO posture must be operational, not cosmetic. The buyer journey, copy, checkout, affiliate/collaborator scripts, support replies, invoices, labels, SDS/COA docs, and AI-readable files must all support the same non-human-use stance.
  3. Bulgaria OpCo is an infrastructure hypothesis until counsel/broker gates clear it. Use Bulgaria EOOD as the leading plan, but require molecule, customs, REACH/CLP, VAT, banking, and payment-processor validation before relying on it.
  4. AI can run the business, but regulated decisions need signed gates. Agents may draft, reconcile, alert, and prepare actions; humans approve money-out, public claims, new suppliers, new SKUs, legal/counsel decisions, imports, payment routing, and live outbound messages.
  5. Accessories are a separate business surface. Keep accessory copy, marketplace listings, stock, claims, and compliance separate from peptides so a low-risk accessory SKU does not inherit RUO peptide risk.

Decision Drivers

  1. Regulatory defensibility: the highest-risk failure is being treated as unauthorized medicine commerce because site, support, affiliate, or buyer behavior contradicts RUO.
  2. Auditability: every SKU, batch, stock move, customer order, payment, affiliate/collaborator payout, internal-use movement, local physical handoff, COA, and exception must be reconstructable without chat archaeology.
  3. Margin-realistic automation: automation should enforce pricing, COA, VAT, marketing-reserve, and stock gates; it must not accelerate unprofitable or non-compliant sales.

Options

Option A: Controlled RUO D2C plus B2B transition

Summary: Keep individual sales for cashflow while hardening the RUO controls and building institutional/B2B as the long-term defensible channel.

Pros: - Preserves current revenue and proof-of-demand. - Matches the Monday control package decision: hybrid now, institutional B2B in parallel. - Allows gradual claims/checkout/affiliate tightening without killing operations.

Cons: - Still carries D2C posture risk because broad individual buyers weaken RUO defensibility. - Requires rigorous monitoring of support, affiliates, ads, and checkout behavior. - Payment processors and marketplaces may still classify the activity as high-risk.

Risk: D2C cashflow pressure causes exceptions to become the norm.

Option B: Institutional-only RUO reset

Summary: Stop broad individual sales, sell only to verified labs/institutions/business researchers, and rebuild copy/checkout around qualification.

Pros: - Best legal posture under Topic D findings. - Cleaner documentation story for counsel, banks, payment processors, and customs. - Easier to justify COA/SDS/batch paperwork and account-level qualification.

Cons: - Likely slower revenue and harder acquisition. - Current customer network and affiliate plans become mostly unusable. - Requires a real B2B sales process and institutional verification workflow.

Risk: Business stalls before B2B demand is proven.

Option C: Accessories-first compliance bridge

Summary: Freeze peptide scaling, build accessory commerce first, and use accessories revenue to fund legal, COA, and AI ops.

Pros: - Lower regulatory load for storage/organization/cold-chain accessories if claims stay clean. - Useful marketplace channel validation for eMAG/Amazon before peptide-related scaling. - Can build logistics, packaging, QC, returns, and marketplace skills without peptide claims.

Cons: - Does not solve peptide compliance by itself. - Some accessories are still medical-adjacent: pens, cartridges, sterile vials, filters, BAC water, alcohol prep pads. - Needs separate supplier/QC and marketplace policy checks.

Risk: Accessory catalog drifts into administration/injection-use support and imports peptide risk by implication.

ADR

Decision: Choose Option A as the primary business path, with Option B as the 6-month target posture and Option C as a parallel low-risk cashflow/logistics track.

Rationale: - The project already locked a hybrid posture: keep current individual sales for cashflow while building B2B and hardening RUO controls. - Immediate institutional-only reset is cleaner legally but may be commercially premature. - Accessories are useful, but only as a separate track; they cannot be used to sanitize peptide claims or sell administration workflows.

Consequences: - D2C sales continue only through tighter gates: claim-clean site, buyer acknowledgement, COA status, support-script control, no dosage/use advice, and affiliate freeze until counsel/site cleanup. - B2B account workflow becomes a required build item, not a future nice-to-have. - Bulgaria EOOD setup becomes a gated infrastructure project, not a casual incorporation step. - AI automation focuses first on audit/control, then operational execution, then limited autonomous actions.

Follow-ups: - Get DGKV Sofia/BG pharma counsel and Gerlach/customs broker quotes. - Convert the site from consumer commerce language to research-supply workflow. - Build canonical SKU/batch/customer/order/stock/COA/exception schema. - Prove accessories order, QC samples, and marketplace eligibility before listing.
