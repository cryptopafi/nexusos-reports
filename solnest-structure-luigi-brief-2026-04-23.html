Why this matters

Italy is the most aggressive country in the European Union at asserting authority over foreign platforms. In 2023, Airbnb Ireland settled with the Italian tax authorities for €576 million because Italy ruled Airbnb had failed to collect a 21% withholding on short-term rentals made through its platform — despite the contracting entity being Irish. In 2020, the Italian operation of Uber Eats was placed under judicial administration by Milan prosecutors over driver-classification issues.

The lesson is clear: "we are Irish, Italian rules do not apply to us" is not a viable long-term structure. Every marketplace that scales in Italy ends up with some form of declared Italian presence. The question is not whether, but when and how.

Why not the cheaper options?

We also modelled an Ireland-only setup with Italian contractors and two Estonia variants. Both save real money on paper but concentrate risk precisely where it hurts most — at Series A due diligence — through two exposures: permanent-establishment (PE) treatment by Italian tax authorities, and contractor-to-employee reclassification by Italian labour courts.

The Airbnb and Uber precedents show these setups do not survive scale. Italian tax authorities and labour inspectors reach through the foreign-parent structure the moment there is meaningful commercial activity on Italian soil. At the €300,000 SAFE stage, the risk discount a serious Series A investor would apply to a contractor-only Italian setup exceeds the annual tax-efficiency premium.

Estonia specifically performs worse than Ireland on founder extraction. At €200,000 per year in distributed profits, the total tax leakage is 35.3% through Ireland versus 42.3% through Estonia — Ireland wins by €14,000 per year per founder.
