1. Step-Back Answer: Minimum Legal Presence Italy Requires

Question: For a digital marketplace earning commission on solar-installation leads in Italy, what are the MINIMUM legal presences Italy requires — by law, not by convention?

Answer (by law, not convention): Zero Italian legal entity. Zero Italian payroll. Zero Italian office. What Italy actually requires is compliance with four obligations (not entities):

  1. Tax obligations: VAT compliance for any Italy-source taxable supplies. Depending on model, this is either (a) OSS declaration from Ireland for B2C digital services, (b) reverse-charge mechanism for B2B commission to IT installers (installer self-assesses IT IVA, Solnest invoices 0% VAT), or (c) IT Partita IVA direct registration IF the platform is deemed “presumed supplier” under Art. 14a (triggered by payment facilitation + key operational control). Option (b) is cleanest and likely applies to pure commission-on-lead model. No Italian legal entity required in any of three scenarios.
  2. Consumer law obligations: Codice del Consumo (D.Lgs. 206/2005) applies to Italian consumers regardless of platform’s country of establishment (Rome I Art. 6 — consumer habitual residence rule). IE HoldCo must: (i) provide IT-language T&Cs, (ii) honor 14-day withdrawal right on distance contracts, (iii) provide Italian-language complaint handling, (iv) comply with Dispute Resolution Directive 2013/11/EU. No “in-country representative” required. Jurisdictional submission (accepting IT court jurisdiction for IT consumers) is standard and does NOT create PE.
  3. Data protection: GDPR (IE lead supervisory authority = Data Protection Commission). IT Garante can claim concurrent jurisdiction under Art. 56 GDPR one-stop-shop exception for “local processing” claims from IT data subjects. Appoint DPO under GDPR Art. 37 if required by processing scale; no IT rep required (Art. 27 GDPR applies only to non-EU controllers).
  4. DAC7 reporting: IE platform operator reports IT-resident sellers (installers) to Irish Revenue; AEOI pushes data to AdE. No direct filing to AdE. Filed from Ireland.

What Italy absolutely does NOT require: Italian subsidiary, Italian permanent establishment (unless triggered by conduct), Italian director, Italian payroll, Italian bank account, Italian commercial registration (Camera di Commercio / REA), Italian INPS/INAIL, Italian VAT rep (Art. 17 D.P.R. 633/72 reps are for non-EU companies; IE is EU). Italian solar-sector licensing (GSE/Terna/ARERA) does not apply to pure digital intermediaries.

Bottom line: The “IT SRL is required” narrative of the morning analysis was grant-driven, not law-driven. Remove grants, remove SRL. Italy welcomes EU-established digital platforms via the Services Directive 2006/123/EC and E-Commerce Directive 2000/31/EC (country-of-origin principle for information society services).

3. PE Risk Memo (under 600 words)

Scope: What 3 actions are most likely to trigger deemed IT Permanent Establishment for IE HoldCo, and how to structure around each?

Legal baseline: Italy-Ireland DTA Art. 5 (OECD Model aligned). Italy’s Tax Police Circular 1/2018 is the enforcement playbook, reinforced by TUIR Art. 162 (as amended 2018 implementing OECD Action 7 dependent-agent extension + 2024 digital reforms). Tax Ruling 193/2025 (AdE) confirms digital “significant economic presence” applies to VAT scope, NOT to IRES/CIT PE under DTA — DTA prevails.

Action 1 — MGC referral fees (HIGHEST RISK)

Mechanism: Partner owns MGC (Italian installer) + 15% SAFE in IE HoldCo. MGC receives referral fees from IE HoldCo for introducing customers. If MGC personnel habitually negotiate installer onboarding or “play decisive role” in closing supplier contracts on behalf of IE HoldCo, AdE will argue dependent-agent PE under Art. 5(5) DTA and TUIR 162.

Triggers (any one is fatal): - MGC staff conducting supplier recruitment calls using IE HoldCo business cards. - MGC negotiating commission rates with other IT installers on behalf of Solnest. - MGC’s role economically indistinguishable from an employee of Solnest. - Exclusivity clauses making MGC dependent (single-client revenue share).

Structural fix: 1. Written introducer agreement: MGC is a non-exclusive marketing introducer with flat-fee-per-qualified-lead, not a commission-share on closed contracts. 2. No binding authority: Contract language states MGC has zero authority to bind IE HoldCo; all installer contracts executed in Dublin (or via IE-hosted e-signature). 3. Arm’s-length fee: TP-documented referral rate matching 3rd-party rates (€50–200/qualified-lead typical in EU solar lead-gen). 4. Revenue ceiling: MGC referrals < 30% of MGC’s gross revenue — keeps MGC “independent agent” under Art. 5(6) OECD safe harbor. 5. Explicit plural-client: MGC is free to work with competing marketplaces; document it. 6. Arm’s-length installer relationship: MGC must NOT receive preferential commission rates as Solnest supplier (would trigger related-party + dependent-agent combo).

Action 2 — IT marketing agency ad spend (MEDIUM RISK)

Mechanism: IE HoldCo hires IT marketing agency for Meta/Google campaigns in IT language. If the agency’s role is purely promotional (creative production, media buying, analytics), Art. 5(4) OECD preparatory/auxiliary exclusion applies — no PE. Risk escalates if agency personnel start negotiating deals, running customer support, or acting as customer-facing “Solnest Italy” brand-representative.

Fix: Standard MSA with clear scope (ads + creative only), multi-client agency (not single-tenant), no “Solnest Italia” email addresses for agency staff, no customer-facing role.

Action 3 — Founder travel / management-in-Italy (LOW-MEDIUM RISK)

Mechanism: Pafi/Leo conducting IE HoldCo board-level decisions from Italy. This is esterovestizione, not PE per se — but AdE can argue “place of effective management” is Italy, making the whole IE entity IT-resident (worse than PE).

Fix: Real IE-resident non-executive director, real Dublin board meetings with minutes, board resolutions signed in IE, Dublin-resolved strategic decisions. (Already covered in Cortex f6f96196 analysis.)

Verdict: PE risk is manageable but concentrated on MGC. The MGC relationship must be restructured to arm’s-length introducer status, NOT a quasi-exclusive referral partner with dependent-agent optics. This is the single biggest risk item in the IE-only architecture.

4. VAT / IVA Roadmap

Day 1 (Pre-revenue / MVP)

  • Irish VAT registration (mandatory once turnover reaches €37.5K services threshold; register earlier voluntarily to claim input VAT on build costs).
  • OSS (Union One-Stop-Shop) registration in Ireland — only required if providing B2C digital services to IT consumers > €10K combined across EU Member States. For pure B2B commission model (invoicing IT installers), OSS is not needed — reverse-charge applies.
  • B2B invoicing: Solnest invoices IT installer with 0% VAT, note “reverse charge per Art. 44 VAT Directive / Art. 7-ter D.P.R. 633/72”. Installer self-assesses IT IVA.
  • No Italian Partita IVA required unless presumed-supplier Art. 14a triggers (it should not for pure lead-generation commission).

Month 12 / post-€10K B2C threshold

  • If Solnest adds any B2C digital service to Italian consumers (e.g., paid premium listings, consumer-facing subscription, paid advice) AND total cross-border B2C digital sales > €10K — OSS filing from Ireland becomes mandatory. Quarterly OSS declaration includes IT VAT 22%.
  • Still no IT Partita IVA.

Year 2+ / scaling (if presumed-supplier kicks in)

Trigger conditions for presumed-supplier Art. 14a VAT Directive: - Platform facilitates payment AND - Platform controls T&Cs of the transaction OR - Platform is involved in ordering/delivery.

If triggered: Solnest is deemed supplier of the underlying service (installer’s service) to the consumer. Declares full-transaction IT VAT via OSS. No IT direct registration needed as long as OSS covers it. Commission from installer becomes a separate B2B transaction (reverse-charge).

If avoided (via Stripe Connect architecture — platform does NOT handle funds): Art. 14a does not trigger. Simpler.

Year 3+ / material IT presence

If Solnest ever opens an IT office, hires an IT employee, or warehouses any IT assets — IT Partita IVA direct registration becomes mandatory + PE crystallizes. At that point, IT SRL makes sense.

Revenue flow

  1. Italian consumer submits lead on Solnest.ai (IE-hosted platform).
  2. IE HoldCo matches lead to IT installer.
  3. Installer contracts directly with consumer (invoice: installer → consumer, IT VAT 10% or 22% as applicable).
  4. Installer pays Solnest commission (e.g., 8–12% of contract value) via Stripe Connect — reverse-charge B2B invoice from IE.
  5. Stripe Connect holds funds (not Solnest), forwards installer’s net to installer, commission to IE HoldCo.
  6. IE HoldCo books commission as trading revenue @ 12.5% IE CIT.
  7. Platform IP profits may qualify for KDB 10% effective rate with qualifying expenditure.

MGC referral flow

  • MGC introduces consumer leads OR supplier leads to IE HoldCo.
  • IE HoldCo pays MGC fixed fee per qualified lead (e.g., €75 consumer-lead, €500 installer-onboard).
  • MGC invoices IE HoldCo B2B with 0% VAT (reverse-charge to IE; IE HoldCo self-assesses IE VAT 23%).
  • MGC is NOT a Solnest installer on preferential terms.
  • MGC has < 30% of revenue from Solnest (documented annually).

IT marketing agency flow

  • IE HoldCo hires IT agency for paid media + creative (scope: ads only, no customer-facing role).
  • Agency invoices IE HoldCo B2B with 0% VAT (reverse-charge to IE).
  • Agency has multiple clients; no exclusivity; no “Solnest Italia” representation.

Threshold at which IT SRL becomes advisable

  • €500K–1M annual IT commission revenue → benefit of local commercialista + IT bank account marginally outweighs IE-only efficiency. Still optional.
  • €3–5M GMV + material IT employee hiring → IT branch (not SRL) is cheaper than SRL and creates PE cleanly without full dual-entity structure.
  • Grants reinstated OR IT-specific regulatory license required → IT SRL mandatory.
  • Series A with IT lead investor requesting IT entity for local governance → case-by-case; push back unless material.
